Invisible Mountains: Ethiopia Part 3

in Uncategorized

on 25 February 2015

If it hadn’t been for Charles who convinced us that the weather in the Simien Mountains might be good, and if it hadn’t been for our desire to do a mountain trek, and finally, if I hadn’t forgotten that the probable weather conditions in the Simien Mountains in July were the main reason why I gave up the idea of going to Ethiopia six years before, if…

If it hadn’t been for all the above, we wouldn’t have lived through those cold and wet days when I was absolutely fed up and I only dreamt of escaping somewhere far, far away from the Siemen Mountains.
It’s only now that I think of that time as a wonderful adventure.

Some of you might think that when I go to places less travelled all I experience is hardship, cultural survival and difficulties. Well, if you think like that, I must tell you, you can’t be further away from the truth – if you knew me better, you’d know that I like relaxation and comfort.
So, bearing that in mind, you shouldn’t be surprised when I say that because of our fear of the rainy season instead of mules we opted for a car. And not only we had a scout (a security guy – that was obligatory), but also a guide, a cook and a cook assistant. And then there were the ladies (us).

We stopped at the viewpoint – it was on of the best views in the days to come and one of the very few moments without rain.

When a truck that we were dragging behind turned over on its side in the middle of the road, we got out and walked through the meadows of extremely green grass.
When another car got stuck in the mud just at a village road turn, we got out again and just walked ahead. We kept walking for a long time, the drizzle started, then it turned to a shower and finally it simply rained. The fog enveilled us and we were longingly looking behind to check if they car was coming. It wasn’t and we were getting wet. The fog was thick and there was no sense in taking the camera out, only sometimes stronger gusts of wind would scatter the fog and we what we saw then made us regret we couldn’t see more.


Once in the shelter we went to the toilet. We went together.
“Shit, which way now?” – mumbled someone when we suddenly stopped not being able to see anything 2 meters away.
With a drizzle, making a fire outside was impossible, so the cook went in and began dinner preparations. He did it at the African pace. The darkness came, the smells became more intense, but it still looked like like a long way to go before we would fill our stomachs with food.
It was still raining outside and the humidity got inside making me shiver with cold at the table. I had two pairs of socks on and thermal underwear but I still felt cold. In fact I put on everything I could.
Three long hours of waiting and the dinner was ready. It was simply delicious. I guess we would have enjoyed anything warm, but the cook really put his heart into it.

In beds we tucked ourselves into our sleeping bags hoping they would protect us against cold and fleas (if there were any). On the brink of falling asleep we heard screeching of the door and steps inside, a woman entered, made a loud thud with something she was holding, put it on the floor and left.
“What’s that?” I heard the question and squeaking of floor as one of my friends was trying to find a flashlight. A moment later, in the dim light we saw a plastic potty!

Someone bolted the door and everybody fell asleep. A few hours later, with the darkness still outside our windows and rain drops hammering the metal room I was woken to the sound of chopping and clanking of pots. An hour later a smell reached my bed, yet another hour and it was time to get up – breakfast was ready!

The cook (left) and a cook assistant (right) who was working really hard.

It wasn’t just training, it was pouring down and yet we set off to get to the waterfall. No fleas at night, warm breakfast – it all made for a good day start. Our neighbour who spent the night in tents also seemed to be in good mood.
The road was slimy and mud would stick to our soles making the boots weigh twice as much. I admired the scout who walked in sandals and whitish socks which seemed to be clean and dry.

Scaut – a really nice man though he didn’t spoke a word to us. He’s wearing plastic sandals which are typical for Ethiopia. 

Return from the trek.
We were dreaming of good coffee, warm shower and clean dry beds.
No electricity in the whole town.
No hot water because it’s impossible to heat it without electricity.
No cold water because it’s impossible to pump it up to the second floor without electricity.
No coffee as the coffee machine needs electricity to work.
No coffee, no good. In fact it’s really bad.

When the electricity returned we decided who dries socks first: one person was doing just that and the other two were stretching hands towards the hot air. Through the noise of a hairdryer I heard a weak knocking on the door – it’s our guide who’s looking for us. We didn’t feel like going out, but he managed to convince us talking about great honey wine. Indeed, the wine was worth it, and guide’s friends were really funny.

In a local bar – it might not look too inviting but it was a place with the right atmosphere. Glass containers on the table are wine glasses, the ones on the floor a wine bottles.
