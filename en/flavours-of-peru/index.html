Flavours of Peru

in Uncategorized

on 26 April 2016

Ceviche

Raw fish, chopped or cut into cubes, cured in citrus juice (usually lemon or lime juice). It is spiced with chilli peppers and often served with onion and cilantro. Two-centimeters’ cubes should be marinated for about three hours, but if it is finely chopped it can be served almost immediatelly after preparation.
My personal preference was for finally chopped ceviche.

Potatoes

Peru is the birthbplace of potatoes – it is where the crop originated over 5000 years BC. If you go to Peru, I recommend a visit to a supermarket or a local market to see how many different kinds of potatoes there are.
Some of the shapes and flavours might be pretty unusual.

Cuy (Guinea pig)

Peruvians eat over 65 million guinea pigs annually. This this is so popular that you can even see it painted on the Peruvian version of the Last Supper!
Most tourists will wince at the sight of a dead body of a guinea pig on a plate, but it’s worth trying – the meat is soft and delicate and the only disadvantage is that there isn’t much of it.

Quinoa

It has been grown in Peru and Bolivia for a few thousand years. Recently it has become really popular as super foods in the USA and Europe and it led to the increase of prices on the local markets.
It can be eaten plain (as a substitute of rice or pasta), in salads and in soups.
I especially like it in soups – I sometimes add it to my vegetable soups.

 
 
 

Chicha morada

Sweet beverage made from Purple corn. Corn is cooked with pineapple, cinnamon, cloves and sugar. It is usually served cold, there are also alcoholic options available, although they’re less common.
It tastes like a kind of fruit juice with corn flavour.

Mate de Coca (Coca Tea)

The cultivation of coca plant is legal in Peru. You can also buy coca leaves in the markets and tea bags in supermarkets. However, made from fresh coca leaves tastes the best, even dried leaves don’t give you the same flavour – so drink it fresh when you can. Remember, you can’t take it over the border (although people often do it). It tastes like a delicate green tea. It is recommended in case of altitude problems and when you are tired as it boosts your energy.
 
 

Sugar Cane Juice

It is extracted from the pressed sugar cane – stop at the market to see how it’s done.
Many tourists dislike the taste saying it’s too bland, however, I truly enjoy drinking it – it’s a bit sugary and refreshing.
 
 
 
 

Inca Kola

Popular Peruvian beverage bright yellow in colour, very sweet, resembles the flavour of bubble gum.
Try it – once is often enough (unless you are a fan of extremelly sweet soft drinks).
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 

Tres Leches Cake

Tres leches means three milks – the cake is popular in many Latin American countries. It’s a sponge cake soaked in three kinds of milk: evaporated milk, condensed milk and heavy cream.
The recipe is easy, it is slightly moist and really delicious.
 
 
 

Chocolate

Some people can’t imagine life without chocolate (I can’t) but it wasn’t until 16th century that Cristopher Columbus brought it to Europe.
We are accustomed to the smooth, delicate flavour of our European chocolates so the chocolate in Latin America might surprise you – just try it!
You can read more about my experience with chocolate making in the post Save the Earth it’s the only planet with chocolate.
