Learn Spanish in… Bolivia!

in Uncategorized

on 24 March 2015

“Studying while on holidays?” many of you will make a face thinking it’s a nonsense, but just consider it for a moment and you might actually come to the conclusion it’s quite a good idea after all.
Learning Spanish in Latin America will not only improve your communication skills, but it’s a magnificent way of having a closer look at alocal community while you chill out staying in aplace for a few more days.

You don’t have time? Don’t worry. A week is more than enough! Or, to be precise, you need five days: Monday to Friday. The classes start in the morning and finish around the midday – lots of time left for sightseeing and entertainment!
Just be careful when you select the place to stay – it’s important that you enjoy it!

If you’re visiting Bolivia I highly recommend studying Spanish in Sucre.
“Why Sucre?” you could ask just the way I asked once. Well, there are a few reasons:
– Sucre is at the altitude of about 2,500 meters above sea level and the climate is moderate – not too hot, not to cold,
– it’s the constitutional capital of Bolivia, so you should visit it anyway,
– you feel safe walking around on your own, and coming back home at night is not a problem,
– it’s popular with foreigners so if you tire of Bolivian cuisine you’ll find many cosy pubs and cafes where you’ll talk with others over an international dish and beer.

Sucre: Day and Night.

Bolivian Spanish School was one of the schools recommended to me, and the only one that quickly answered my emails explaing in detail what I asked about – no surprise then that I decided to study there, right?
The classes usually start with a test, a conversation about your needs and studying plans for the coming week. What I like at schools in Latin America is that there’re mostly one-to-one classes so a teacher’s attention is focused solely on you, your progress and your problems. Some people might be afraid that being the only student in a class is too demanding and boring. Well, it’s not true, they’re not boring at all, because you’re involved all the time. The classes are very useful – you practise all the time and because of that your progress is fast.

My classes started at eight in the morning and every day as I was walking down the the streets I promised myself I would get up earlier the following day and take a photo walk through a town waking up to life. The only problem was that I knew I wouldn’t work out because as much as I love taking photos, getting up earlier is something I can hardly ever force myself to.

Everyday, a few minutes before eight kitchen school would fill up with yawning students quickly preparing their cups of coffee and then rushing to the classrooms.
Two hours later, during the break we were more talkative, the ‘official’ languages being Spanish (for those who worked hard to improve) and English (for those who were just beginners).
We would get to know each other not only during the breaks but also during the classes if we went out with our teachers.

1. My teacher Mirian and my classrom. 2. Telling the fortune at Alasitas fair. 3. A local market.

I really enjoyed those hours spent with Mirian outside the school building – she took me to try the best salteñas in the town, showed round the mask museum or she explained the intricacies of Alasitas festival while answering my numerous questions.

Probably the best salteñas in town!

How to make papas rellenas – after class cooking practice.  

After we studied Spanish verbs, nouns, subjuntivo or else for half a day, the classes ended and went for lunch to our houses. I lived at Ana’s place but we ate lunch in another house where many of her family members gathered at lunchtimes – really great people!
If you intend to learn Spanish, living with a Bolivian family is the best idea – you’ll not only practise your Spanish, but also see how different (or similar) Bolivian lifestyle is from the one you know from home.

My Bolivian family. Ana and me in the first photo. Before I went to to South America some people warned me that Bolivians are rather distant and might be unfriendly – my stay in Sucre proved it was quite the opposite!

I fell in love with old post-colonial houses – the same I’m in love with old tenant houses in Poznań, my hometown! I enjoy houses with souls and a little bit of history. In Spanish houses I enjoy patios and space.

The patio – on the right in the corner is the entrance to my room.

I only regret I hadn’t stayed longer!
