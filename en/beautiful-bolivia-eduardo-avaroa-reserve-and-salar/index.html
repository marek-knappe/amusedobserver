Beautiful Bolivia – Eduardo Avaroa Reserve and Salar

in Uncategorized

on 29 March 2015

Itinerary: Park Eduardo Avaroa + Salar (4 days from Tupiza)

Day 1.

Quebrada de Palala – interesting rock formations. Our first stop on the way. At first I felt disappointed when Fernando, our driver, told us we stop for 15 minutes only – I jumped out of the jeep as fas as I could and… the moment I did it, the fight with the wind began. I was shivering and trying to take photos. Before 15 minutes passed I’d already got into a warm car.

(if you’re interested in practical tips look HERE)

Wind and sand – it’s what the first day was like. Landscaped were obscured by dust, and when we would leave the jeep sunglasses were necessary to protect the eyes. It was better not to speak too much if you didn’t want sand in your mouth.


The saying ‘in the middle of nowhere’  suddenly made sense as we kept on driving. We passed some small villages with several houses and a church. People there raise llamas or work excavating minerals. Once a week a priest comes to celebrate a mass.


Llamas – black, white, brown…

On the way we drove past a ghost-town. Nobody knows why people left one place and one day moved to another. They left their houses behind and they fell into ruins.


The evening has come – the end of sightseeing, time for dinner and sleep.

Day 2.
In the morning we manage to see where we slept (and froze).

We stopped nearby to admire llamas. Aren’t they cute?


We drove though frozen streams and little rivers and suddenly we got stuck.






Looking at the photos you might think it was warm. It couldn’t be further from truth – it was cold! Hats, scarves and gloves were a must.




Laguna Verde – Green Lagoon. The colour comes from minerals in the water. Because of arsenic there is no life in the lagoon.
It’s kind of hard to take a shot of a cold, lonely lagoon – everybody wants to do it at the very same moment.


Hot springs. Who’s brave enough undress and take a dip?

We visited gaysers reaching the highest point during our journey – over 5000 meters above sea level.

Another beautiful lagoon – Laguna Rosada.


It was the last thing to see that day. The sun was still high and everybody walked off to shoot numerous pictures. James and Mathew got bored pretty quickly and decided to take a walk back to our hotel – it was pretty close after all.
When we were just about to leave Fernando asked where they were and looked at us in desbelief when we explained they’d left an hour before. He couldn’t understand why they would to anything like that and looked around the car to make sure we weren’t lying. Fifteen minutes later we saw them on the way – somewhere in the middle of the way. With so huge open spaces distances don’t seem what they really are.

Day 3. Arbol de Piedra – Stone Tree (height around 7 meters)



Cold. I hadn’t been in such low temperatures for a long time – I couldn’t remember the last time I walked on a frozen lake.

In search of flamingos – I didn’t even know they lived in cold at such altitudes. It was one of the few times I regretted I’d left my better camera with zoom lens at home.The red spot in the middle of the lake – yes, these are flamingos.

 


Flat tyre – a chance to take more photos.

Day 4.
Sunrise at the cacti island Incahuasi (quechua: Incahuasi – Inca’s house)  – a rocky island at 3,656 meters above sea level in the middle of Salt Flats.



Everybody left the hotel early to get here before the sunrise. Sunrises look better in real life than in the photos so I didn’t bother with too many photos. I spent my time simply admiring the sun going up and freezing. Oh, what a freezing morning it was!
At the foot of the hill, just in front of the cars you can see salt tables and benches where we had breakfast half an hour later – it was so cold that even hot tea didn’t help much.
.

Salt hotel. 
Built in the early 90s in the middle of Salt Flats. There were 12 double rooms, a toilet but no shower. Its locations caused numerous sanitary problems so it was closed in 2002 to avoid environmental pollution. Another hotel was built on the edge of Salar.

Train Cemetery.
We worried if we would get to see it, because local people blocked roads in the protest against the new location of the bus station. Fortunately, they decided to stop the protest on Sunday, our last day of trip.
I wish we’d had more time there – the place must be a perfect for photo shots at sunrise or sunset.

HERE you can see more photos taken at the Train Cemetery and read its story.


Time to go back. A few more photos before the sun set More winding and narrow roads for the next eight hours on the way back to Tupiza.
