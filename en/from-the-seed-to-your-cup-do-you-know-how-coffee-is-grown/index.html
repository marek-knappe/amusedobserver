From the seed to your cup. Do you know how coffee is grown?

in Uncategorized

on 21 April 2015

Finca is a plantation. Finca Colombia is a coffee plantation that I visited in the Guatemalan town of Antigua at the end of my first Latin American trip. Learning is something is really enjoy so I thought (and I was right) that getting some knowledge about coffee might be an amazing experience.

Why is a Guatemalan finca called Colombia? Nobody knows, not even the plantation owner, Margarita Asensio. When her father, a doctor, decided to get involved in coffee growing and bought a plantation in the 1920s it was already called like that.

For over two hours Margarita entertained us with stories as we followed her footsteps amid the lush greenery.

Cofffee growing in Guatemala is different from Costa Rica or Brasil: coffee bushes are in planted in the shades of bigger trees so that they grow longer and water is longer retained.

There are two types of coffee at Finca Colombia: Bourbon and Caturra – both are varieties of coffee Arabica.

Coffee trees at the plantation are between 1 and 40 years of age – we had a closer look at different types but Margarita told as you need experience to correctly guess the age.

Coffee seed are planted in beds called nurseries:

then seedlings are removed from bed to be planted in individual pots:

After some time they are strong enough to be planted in suitable places – in ordered rows divided by adequate spaces. Margarita explained that a lot had changed since her father took care of the plantation. Together with her sons she decided to introduce some changes: they began planting two seedling in one place, reduced space beteween them, trimmed the trees, used smaller grabiler trees that give shade ana are easier to trim – in case of small trees the workers are paied by a tree (5 quezals per tree) when they work with bigger trees, the workers are paid by the hour.

As trees which are supposed to give shade are planted together with coffee seedling they are of the same size (left photo), but two years later they are twice as big as coffee trees, and after a few more years they are huge and protect coffee trees from the sun (right photo).

When the coffee fruit called coffee cherry turns bright red it means it’s ripe and the time of harvest has come. Harvest time is more or less between December and February – it differs slightly each year. If we’d visited the plantation during that time we would have seen crowds of women picking the crop by hand. The majority of coffee pickers are women because apart from being housewives they lack any other work and coffee harvest is a chance to earn some money. They arrive at the dawn, with children who then play together all day long. During the day they have breaks to make a fire and prepare meals from the food they brought from home.

A hard-working exprienced picker is able to collect up to 90 kilos of coffee beans.

When coffee is collected it must be sorted, cleaned and dried. To understand the processes we went inside the buildings to have a look at the machinery.

Coffee beans are first cleaned in a tank full of water (left photo) and they end up in a machine shown on the right

from where they are moved to big concrete tanks and they leave the building through narrow paths of another concrete construction. When outside they are evenly spread on a concrete patio and the drying process begins.

The coffee that is dried on the patio is everyday collected into piles and covered for the night to prevent them from getting went. Depending to the weather the drying process might last several weeks. It’s important not to mix the coffee picked on different days because the length of the drying process is extremelly important – the process is completed when the beans have the moisture of maximum 12%.

On the left you can see coffee with dried husk that will disappear after the coffee goes through the machine visible on the right.

Hulled coffee looks like that:

This is a good quality coffee that is going to be exported. Margarita exports coffee to Japan.

Then comes the time to remove bad quality beans.
Beans of wrong colour size or otherwise unacceptable are removed but not thrown out. These bad quality seeds are used to produce instant coffee.
Knowing that… are you sure you still want to drink instant coffee?

Defective seeds below:

Coffee is next packed in bags (which are sewn up by hand) and moved to a warehouse when it must rest for some time (just like wine) before it is exported.

The warehouse:

When the harvest is completed, the warehouse fills up, but what we could see in August where just some coffee bags with coffee brewed in Cafe Chi Cua. The cafe was set up a few years ago. When people who came up for a cup of coffee started asking about the possibility of buying some coffee they tried Margarita decided to sell small half a kilo bags. People were also willing to see how coffee grows and this is how coffee plantation tours started in 2007.

Our visit to a coffee plantation was a fascinating exprience. When we were driven back to the centre of Antigua and dropped at Cafe Chi Cua where we tried another cup of coffee I fully aware of what I was drinking and I thought that knowing what I knew the coffee taster really rich.

From the seed to your cup – that’s what the sign on a coffee bean bag says.
