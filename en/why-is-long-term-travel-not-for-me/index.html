Why is long-term travel not for me?

in Uncategorized

on 9 May 2015

Who doesn’t want to travel?

Everybody wants to travel the world and they want to travel long. Or do they?
I’d say it’s the most often repeated travel myth. If you’ve heard it many times it might be just because those who dream of turning into full-time travellers simply keep repeating it, while those who don’t want to ‘give it all up and start travelling’ see no reason to voice their opinions.

Even if your friends complain about their holidays being too short it doesn’t necessarily mean they have a deep desire to conquer the world – they would probably be more than happy with a week or two more. That’s it.
I know many people who consider a month-long trip to be a long one and they aren’t keen on travelling longer at a stretch even though they would have time for that, and money wouldn’t be a big problem either.

I’ve always been quite satisfied with the length of my travels. I wish I could travel more often or had more money to splurge on extras, but travelling long-term has never really appealed to me and the reasons are numerous.

Tygrys w Tajlandii
Lokalny rynek w Boliwii
When I travelled for over two months for the first time I was overwhelemed with everything around me and while I spent lots of time off the beaten track I would still enjoy the most typical tourist attractions, many years later, during another two months’ trip I was a strong afficionado of slow travel.

Is money the only thing that stops you?

First of all, money seems to be the biggest problem. I hope you’re not rushing now with your good advice on how to save money to travel the world, because I guess I’ve saved enough. Travelling however is not the only thing I want to save for and also whatever I’ve managed to save wouldn’t be enough for the comfort I’m used to have at home.

Are you willing to accept the lack of comfort?

Tedious trekking to remote areas without the access to running water, sleeping in a hammock and taking a bath in a coffee-coloured river or riding on crowded long-distance buses with freezing air-con, blaring music or smelly animals is what I consider fascinating adventures, but it’s much more enjoyable if there’s a hot shower or a comfortable bed waiting for me at the final destination.

I can survive without a bathroom, I’m used to walking my feet off in search of affordable accommodation. There have been times when I was too tired to care and I’d stay in the closest place available where I was happy the light was too dim to notice any details around. I’m able to cope with problems, inconveniences and discomfort without letting my spirit down for too long, but I cannot say I find it enjoyable and when it comes to summing it up,  I guess that’s what I want my travels to be: enjoyable.

Travelling on tight budget doesn’t mean sleeping only in bad places. Bad places happen, but there are many good ones  although they require more effort to find.

Is meeting people on the road better than time with your friends at home?

People are the most important or: Meeting people is why I love travelling – many travellers repeat it like a mantra explaining that they set off without a travel companion because then the interaction with local people is much easier.
I won’t deny that travelling alone makes me more open to experiences and I have a stronger desire to interact with people I meet on the road, both travellers and locals – sometimes I can’t believe how many stories you might hear and how many experiences you might share during a single trip. Meeting locals gives you insight into everyday life of a given country, helps you understand behaviours and reactions better and enriches your travels.

What I find discouraging is the realisation that all those people appear in my life for a brief moment only, most of our interactions are superficial and we say goodbye even before we learn more about who those people are. During a short trip, the fast pace of travelling, people coming and going and changing surroundings are attractive, for all we want is to experience as much as we can, in a long run it’s exhausting and I’m sick and tired of repetitive conversations and I miss friends at home.

Left: My friends and I before our trip to Guatemala. It was a lot of fun, always chatting, sharing opinions, enjoying ourselves. We did split sometimes, but we kept together for most of the time.
Right: The Thai family I stayed with for some time – an amazing cultural experience, but there were so many times I regretted I couldn’t communicate with them properly – their English was as bad as my Thai.

How long can you travel without knowing a language?

Many people will probably be surprised now reading that I consider languages an obstacle while travelling. I speak fluent English, my Spanish is very good, I forgot a lot of my French but life proved it’s still good enough to wander around and I think if I were forced I’d brush up my Russian quickly. I’m self-confident even when travelling to the countries where those languages are not widely spoken because I’ve learnt from experience that if you’re persistent (or desperate) you can always communicate.

Sometimes I can’t really decide if I prefer visits to places where I communicate without problems or to countries where communication is an everyday struggle – such places are usually more fun and such challenges make my holidays amazing. My travels are riddled with language misunderstandings, learning and trying. Those known and unknown languages are one of the reasons why I choose some exotic destinations over the beauty of my own country.
Yet, having said all that I need to admit that if I travel for longer than two – three weeks, surviving or “just’ communicating is simply not enough. As the time passes by I long for more meaningful conversations and discussions.

Travelling far and long – is it running away?

Travelling is a kind of therapy for me: all the trouble suddenly seems distant, I enjoy being offline and it amazes me how unnecessary email and Facebook suddenly become, my life slows down and I feel there’s nothing “I must” do. Such a kind of attitude gives me a boost and life seems so much better when I get back home. Coming home is good, because this is the place I belong, I know, I’m interested in.

I’ve met many long-time, long-distance travellers and I was often disappointed how boring they were because although they saw many things they didn’t have time to stop, learn more, they became as superficial as their travels.
I met a few round-the-world travellers I listened to with attention and admiration, but they were not many, so I guess being a good traveller is not an easy job.

on a beach in Belize
Amazon jungle
Being on holiday is absolutely amazing because I so often do things totally different from my everyday activities. The memories after holidays and expectactions before the trips are what keeps me going in my daily life.

Expectations

I hope I’ll never get bored of travelling, of learning, of gathering experiences.
I dream of adventure, I’m happy when I’m faced with unexpected and being outside of my comfort zone for some time feels refreshing. Although I’m not planning on living for months out of my backpack, I hope to explore more varied ways of travelling in the future.
And I do hope I will be able to travel more often.

Somwhere in Norway
The more I travel the more important it is how I travel not where.

For those dreaming about travelling round the world…

My dislike for long-time travel doesn’t stop me from following round-the-world travelling bloggers – if you know any such blogs let me know in the comments!
