How to deal with your friends’ reactions to your travels?

in Uncategorized

on 6 February 2016

It’s the 21st century and you might think the world has shrunk: faraway destinations became closer as we get news and information from every corner of the world. You might think like that, but do others share your point of view?
Why so many people who travel to less obvious and less touristy areas constantly hear questions “why there?”, “Is it safe?”, “Is there anything to see at all?”
How should we react and behave when our family and friends try to advise against travelling to places we want to visit? What to say when they try to make us feel guilty expressing their concerns for our well-being?
Here you’ll find some advice based on my experience and gathered during discussions with fellow travellers (I’ve never done some of them, but there are people saying they work).

1. Tell No One!

Theoretically, it’s the simpliest and the easiest solution. However, it hardly ever works. First because we are a bit vain and we want the world to admire our journeys (don’t we?) and secondly, if something bad happens no one will know – that might be a problem.

When I was going on holidays to Ethiopia I didn’t mention my plans to visit a neighbouring country of Somaliland – everybody would’ve mistaken it with Somalia (where I was definitely not going!)
In the photo: fishermen in Berbera, the Gulf of Aden (Somaliland).  

2. Tell Just a Best Friend (or a few best friends).

Tell the people you trust – it’s really the best if someone knows where you are. Don’t say a word to those who are likely to panic, to any killjoys or any jealous people.

3. Wait Till the Very Last Moment.

No matter how much you’re dying to share your joy and spread the word that you’re going to discover a new unknown (to you) lands, wait till you are about to leave and till you backpack or suitcase are packed – that will give other less time to get nervous.

4. Find Books and Films.

Suggest reading books and watching films that would show the place you’re going to explore. But, be careful! Make sure that the places are presented in a positve way so that it will become clear that you’re going to an ordinary place.

“Stoning of Soraya M” is definitely one of the best films connected with Iran that I’ve seen, but you should never recommend it to people who are afraid of Iran.

5. Tell that You are Going With Somebody.

If the biggest problem seems to be that you’re going on your own, simply tell that you’ve got a travel companion – someone who’s experienced in this kind of trips (it might be a lie, but you’re likely to meet someone on the way, so you might have the company after all!).

6. Make Others Familiar With Your Destination.

Long before your trip start talking about your destination. People are ofen afraid because they lack information and they have wrong ideas about the places – you need to change them! Show the photos! Tell about the friends (Internet friends even), talk about the cuisine – make them familiar with what they’re frightened of.

7. Present a Worse Destination.

So, you’ve said you’re going to destination X and everybody gets nervous? Well, a few days later say you’ve changed your mind and mention place Y, and remember, the new Y place should be much worse. When you finally ‘decide’ that X is much better idea and that’s exactly where you’re going, everybody is going to feel relieved 🙂

8. Prevent Your Family and Friends from Watching TV and Reading Newspapers.

That would be an ideal solution! It’s a pity it’s not really possible to do. Faraway and less touristy places usually hit the headlines when something really good or bad happens. The good things are quickly forgotten, the bad ones are stuck in our memory forevery and make up a negative imagie of a city, region or country.

9. Find Statistics!

Find the statistics that would prove how safe is your future destination. Select carefully – if you are going to dive with shark, don’t say how dangerous it might be, find the figures that will prove that more people die annually taking selfies than in shark attacks (that’s actually true!). Avoid maps that show in colours safe/dangerous countries – usually, a very small part/ region is dangerous (usually border areas), but the whole country is painted (dangerous) red.

Most sharks do not hurt humans: among over 400 species, only a few are responsible for people’s deaths. In the photo: leopard shark (Kho Phi, Phi, Thailand)

10. Tell that You Cannot Avoid Your Fate.

Keep saying that every place might be dangerous, after all, many people die in your country every day, don’t they? Ideally (although I hope it is not true in your case), explain that something bad happened to you in your familiar surroundings. Whenever my friends come up with all possible black scenarios of what is awaiting a woman travelling solo, I remind them that I was actually mugged close to my home – that usually ends much of the discussions.

11. Find New Friends.

It’s not good to be surrounded with negative people who find faults in everything you want to do or try to stop you from doing things you crave for – is it worth being angry, feeling not understood or annoyed? You cannot change the family – you need to live with them the way they are, some friends are also good although sometimes they don’t react the way you’d like, but why don’t you find some more people similar to you? It’s going to be much easier when you can share your dreams with others. You are not strange because you want to travel – make friends with other travellers!

12. Don’t Talk About Bad Things that Have Happened in Your Other Trips.

I know, I know – you’ve had this life-threatening experience, you’ve survived and you want to be considered a hero. But is it worth putting more stress on your family? Remember, the less they know, the less they fear. And one thing for sure: don’t write about bad stuff when your journey is still going on – they thing in some wild, scary place, they are going to be 100% sure, they are right if you tell them about something wrong! (Well, unless you need rescue – then cry for help immediatelly!)

“Mom, I’m going down the Death Road tomorrow.” I said in reply to my mother’s question how the journey was going on. I didn’t even finished the sentence when I realised that was not a good idea to use the word ‘death’ in my answer.

13. Take Your Parents On a Trip.

If your parents are unsure of you survival skills in a big, big world, take them on a trip to prove that you’re a responsible, organized person who will manage everywhere and can take care of everything.

14. Be an Expert.

Don’t let anyone surprise you with the news from the place where you’re going. Make sure you know what is happening there at the moment. Be familiar with political, social and weather situation – let them know you’re prepared, you are aware, you won’t be surprised. Show you understand their fears, but say your knowledge and preparation is sufficient.

15. Small Steps Method.

Don’t start your travelling by going to the edge of the world, start with your surroundings and each time go further and further. This method is good for reassuring both your family and yourself (if you’re a newbie traveller).

So, what do you think of the methods I’ve discussed here? Have you tried any of them? Have they worked?
Or, if you a worried family member or a friend who is reading my entry: is there anything that could reassure that your near and dear ones are safe somewhere there in the world?

What could be said to the travellers fighting with negative attitudes of other people?
Stick to your plans, defend your choices, don’t get into arguments.
Go, come back safe and tell stories about your fabulous travels!
