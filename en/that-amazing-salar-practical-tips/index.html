That Amazing Salar – practical tips.

in Uncategorized

on 29 March 2015

Salar de Uyuni was my dream destination. I went on holidays with high expectations and fears that reality wouldn’t come up to them.


Some Practical Tips for visiting Salar in July.
The fact that I visited Salar in July is a big importance here as July and August are the coldest months of the year.

If you want just to see some beautiful landscapes go to another entry HERE.

Most trips leave either from Uyuni or from Tupiza. I don’t usually prepare too much before me trip, but this was the special one so I searched the net to find out which would be the best starting point.

Uyuni:
+ lower prices  – 3 days usually, price around 100USD (4 days: 150USD)
–  more people in a jeep (6 people + driver)
– the highlight of the trip – Salar is at the very beginning
– many negative reviews of companies on the Internet

Tupiza:
– more expensive trips, the cost of my 4 day trip was 210USD (we checked around 8 agencies, the price range was between 180-240 dollars)
– less choice
+ no more than four tourists in a jeep + a driver + a cook
+ Salar is at the very end of the trip
+ the town of Tupiza is much more pleasant than Uyuni

Conclusion:

Tupiza is a better starting point: after three days of hardship you finally reach your destination: Salar. Those three days of expectation are part of the adventure. Start in Uyuni? No sense at all – it would be like taking the Inca Trail after you’ve seen Machu Picchu.

– that was the final argument to convince me!

By the way, even if I decided to start in Uyuni it wouldn’t be possible then – local people of Uyuni protested against the location of a new bus station and blocked all exit road.
Strikes, parades and roadblocks are something you need to take into account when in Bolivia so you better always have some extra time in your itinerary.

Weather
July and August are the coldest moths of the year in Bolivian Altiplano. It’s sunny during the day but the wind is strong, at night the temperature might fall as low as minus 15-20 degrees Celcius.

Clothes
Wearing layers is the best idea. It’s hot and stuffy in a car when you ride long distances. Every time you stop you need to put on your jumper, jacket, hat, scarf, gloves.
“Zip up your jackets, it’s very cold,” Fernando kept repeating each time he stopped the car before anyone even managed to open the door.

That’s what I wore everyday: a warm down jacket and alpaca hat and scarf that I bought in La Paz.

Change clothes were not really necessary. I took my jacket off only when I was sitting on the bed ready to dive into my sleeping bag so that I wouldn’t be cold for longer that necessary. Some people slept wearing their hats and scarves.
Every morning I forced myself to brush my teeth and rub my eyes with ice-cold water – the water temperature was definitely something that woke you up so early in the morning.
Warm shower was available only on the last night when we stayed at a salt hotel. There was one shower for the hotel full of people – I waited three hours for my turn. My companions decided that one more night without a shower didn’t really matter, “You’re sure you want to take a shower?” Oh yes, I didn’t. Not just to wash myself but simple to stand under the stream of warm water.

Sleeping bag.
I had my own down sleeping bag. This and three blankets were enough to keep me warm at night.
You can rent a sleeping bag from the agency – I think that in July or August is necessary.

Hotel. The second, coldest day – the picture is blurred because there were problems with electricity and that’s all I managed to get with my camera. Very simple accommodation but it was enough for us. 

Things you should have:
– sunglasses
– sunscreen
– torch
– toilet paper
– extra battery for your camera (or a powerbank)
– extra snacks (sweets, chocolate bars)
– coca leaves for chewing if you’re afraid of the altitude (I don’t like them)
– high altitude sickness pills (Spanish: soroche pills) – during the trip you reach over 5,000 meters above sea level and many people suffer from altitude sickness. My group was lucky but I met some tourists that suffered (headaches, nausea, throwing up). Nobody in my group experienced any problems. I suffered from altitude sickness a few weeks before in Peru and it was a horrible experience that could easily spoil your day so I’d recommend to consider some precautions.

Meals
There’s a driver and a cook for every car. We shared a cook between two cars because James joined us in the last moment (we got a discount because of that). The meals were quite alright but nothing to write home about. We ate lunch on the way, always looking for a place where we could hide from the wind.

Dinner. Even inside we wouldn’t dare taking off hats, scarves and gloves. Well, I only took my gloves off if I couldn’t eat with them on. 

Lunch time! – Can you see the plates? 🙂  

Electricity.
Sometimes we had it sometimes not – torches were really useful. Electricity was not good enough to charge your camera or phone. Only the last night, when we stayed at a salt hotel we could charge our devices.
I have a mirrorless camera that won’t survive long on one battery – an energy powerbank was a must.

Price includes:
– meals and drinks
– accommodation
– transport

Price does NOT include:
– obligatory entrance ticket to Eduardo Avaroa Natural Reserve (about 20 USD) – keep it, it’ll be check later on
– alcohol (nice to drink something on the last day in a salt hotel – a place which is much warmer than previous accommodations and you can simply relax)
– a sleeping bag (rent it from the agency)
– an English-speaking guide – if you want a guide the price is around 100 US dollars. Our driver didn’t speak English at all, but if you have a guide, there’s less space in the car.

The end of the trip
The trip finishes in Uyuni on the fourth day in the afternoon. Most people decide to take a bus to La Paz.
In my group only three people out of nine decided to go back to Tupiza (we were not charged more) and we reached Tupiza around 10 in the evening.

Was the trip worth the price?
Definitely! The tour that we booked with LaTorre Tours was really well organised and I have absolutely nothing to complain about. Fernando, our driver tried to explain some things on the way and patiently answered all our questions (it’s good to now a bit Spanish), and our cook took care that we were neither hungry or thirsty.

Salar at sunrise.

Breathtaking landscapes, lots of wonderful memories and freezing cold.
The dream has come true – I’ve seen Salar de Uyuni!
