Chilean cuisine – a cooking class in Valparaiso.

in Uncategorized

on 26 January 2015

A cooking class is one of many fascinating activities that you may do while travelling: you have fun, you meet people, you learn something new and… last but not least – you know what to cook for your friends back home when your holidays are over.

Do not assume it’s just for cooking lovers, it is not. Cooking together is fun and you’re supervised by a specialist who takes care of the process so that you will not fail and you’ll steer clear of cooking disasters. I met some people who participated in such a class only because they partner or friend wanted to do it, and what they thought would be sacrifice on their part always turned out to be fun.

So, when I found information about a cooking class in Valparaiso I couldn’t miss it!

You usually start with a visit to a local market.
I love local markets! It’s indispensable part of my travelling and a fascinating element of each town and city. I hardly ever miss a chance of strolling among stalls selling fresh produce – I enjoy trying dishes at local eateries where you nudge your neighbour every time you put a fork up to your mouth. I can’t help taking hundreds of photos of everything that’s around. I note down foreign names of products and ask questions when I have no idea what I am looking at. Sometimes I buy unknown things just to experience a new taste.
However, the best market shopping is when you actually need to buy lots of things, when you walk from one stall to the other selecting the best items – and this is exactly what we did with Ines of Chilean Cuisine Cooking Class at the beginning of our cooking course. An hour before we’d sat down at a table to decide on a menu that would satisfy everybody – easier than you’d think – that’s what happens when you meet people eager to try new flavours.

Photo 1: A man filleting a fish we chose for ceviche dish.
Photo 2: Among bananas, sweet potatoes, avocados and corn you can see green chirimoya – a fruit we needed for a dessert.
Photo 3: A seller is cutting off some pumpkin for us.

Ines started our class in a way that was hard not to enjoy – with Chilean wine tasting! We were given four different types of wine and we had to choose two that would accompany our meal. “Do I really need to spit it out?” – someone asked disappointed.

Menu:
ceviche – raw fish or seafood cured in lime or lemon juice;
empanadas – stuffed pastry typical for many Latin American countries,
pebre – chilean sauce made of finely chopped onion, tomatoes, coriander and aji peppers,
charquican – beef stew with potatoes, pumpkin and corn,
ensalada chilea – sliced tomatoes and onions
chirimoya alegre – cherimoya fruit with orange juice,
pisco sour – a coctail typical of Peru and Chile, 35% pisco is the base, you also add lime juice and raw egg white.

We put on our aprons (I loved the school’s aprons in the colours of the Chilean flag!), donned chef’s hats, grabbed the knives and got down to business.

I finally learnt how to cut corn. It’s amazing how everyday you might learn some simple things. My excuse for learning it so late in life is that I don’t actually like corn and I never feel a need to cook it or cut it.

Ceviche was first on our list – it’s a popular dish in coastal areas of Latin America. You use raw fish or seafood to make it and you may cut it into large chunks or chop finely – the bigger the pieces, the longer you need to keep it in a lemon marinade. Due to the duration of the course we had to slice the fish really finely.
Having eaten ceviche in a few restaurants in Mexico, Peru or Chile where it was served chopped into bigger chunks, I must admit that the ceviche that we made was the best, because small parts made it more comfortable to eat and I loved strong citrus flavour.

Preparation of our own empanadas was very imporatant part of our class. As you can see in the photos below it was not an easy task to stuff and shape our empanadas and we had to struggle a bit not to leave any holes in the dough – we managed!
Empanadas are a typical snack that you might buy at many street stalls, but if you make them yourself they taste much better.

Salsa pebre – chilean sauce of onions, tomatoes spiced with coriander and smoked chili pepper is a vital part of the Chilean cuisine – you might eat it just with bread or add to other dishes.
Not everybody liked coriadner, so we prepared two versions: with and without coriander.
Dark brown spice that you can see in a small bowl is called merquen – a wonderful Chilean condiment of mixed spices (smoked chilli pepper as the main ingredient).

If wine is not enough, or if you’re not a wine-lover, don’t worry, if you’re in Chile you can always take PISCO SOUR – a coctail commonly drunk both in Peru in Chile, and if you only start a conversation with a native they’ll always try to convince you it’s their national drink. Pisco is a strong alcohol (around 35%), but the addition of lime juice and raw egg white make it a wonderful, tasty coctail.
Even if you shudder at the thought of adding raw egg white, try it – if you do, you’ll probably love the taste and you’ll drink many more throughout your stay.

The most awaited moment of all cooking classes is when you’ve done everything, and you can just sit down at the table, pose for a photo and start eating while having a pleasant conversation with your companions when you all pride yourselves on a great meal you’ve just prepared.
And this is exactly what happened at the end of our cooking class with Ines.

If you every get the chance of participating in a cooking class during your travels, don’t hesitate, go for it!
And if you’ve already participated in a good quality course, I’m sure you know what I’m talking about, don’t you?
